# The 6-Layer Problem Definition Framework

## Origin & Attribution

This framework was developed by **Teresa Torres** as part of her continuous discovery practice. It is described in detail in [*Continuous Discovery Habits*](https://www.producttalk.org/continuous-discovery-habits/) (2021) and her ongoing work at [producttalk.org](https://www.producttalk.org/).

The framework operationalizes a core insight from Torres: most product teams jump to solutions before they fully understand the problem from multiple angles. The 6 layers force you to look at the same problem through six distinct lenses before any solution work begins.

---

## The Six Layers

| Layer | Core Question | What You're Looking For |
|-------|--------------|------------------------|
| **1 — Customer Mental Models** | What problem does the customer say they have, in their own words? | The language customers use, not your interpretation of it |
| **2 — Ecosystem View** | How do other actors interpret or feel the impact of this problem? | Partners, competitors, adjacent roles — anyone affected beyond your direct user |
| **3 — Behavioral Dynamics** | What frictions, incentives, norms, habits, or power dynamics block or reinforce current behaviors? | The invisible forces that explain why people act the way they do |
| **4 — Status Quo Attempts** | What have people already tried? | Prior solutions, workarounds, abandoned attempts, and why they failed |
| **5 — Technology Enablers** | Where does product or technology create new possibilities? | What is now technically possible that wasn't before |
| **6 — Feasible Influence** | What can we realistically change? | The gap between what's true and what your team can actually move |

---

## Why Six Layers?

Each layer catches blind spots the others miss:

- **Layer 1** captures customer voice but can be misled by what customers *say* vs. what they *do*
- **Layer 2** reveals stakeholder dynamics invisible in customer interviews
- **Layer 3** explains the behavioral gap between stated intent and actual behavior — why people say they want something but don't use it
- **Layer 4** prevents rebuilding failed solutions; forces learning from the graveyard
- **Layer 5** grounds ideation in what's actually possible today
- **Layer 6** filters everything through the lens of what your team can realistically influence, preventing elegant-but-unachievable strategies

Running all six before considering solutions produces a problem definition robust enough to survive contact with reality.

---

## When to Run This Framework

**Run the full 6 layers when:**
- Starting a new product or a significant new feature area
- Repositioning an existing product after stagnation
- A market or competitive shift requires re-grounding in customer reality
- You have a hypothesis but want to stress-test it before committing resources

**Run selected layers when:**
- You have fresh Layer 1–3 data but need to update your status quo scan (Layer 4)
- A new technology shift requires Layer 5 reassessment
- Strategy is drifting from what's achievable (Layer 6 recalibration)

**Skip this framework when:**
- You are executing a known, validated solution — this is a discovery tool, not an execution tool
- You need an answer in 24 hours — each layer deserves at least a few days of research

---

## Depth Modes

This skill supports two research depths. Choose before starting:

### Quick Mode (~1–2 days per layer)
- Lean on synthesis documents and existing desk research
- 5–10 sources per layer
- Produces a synthesis document per layer + a cross-layer summary
- Best for: repositioning or layer updates on an existing product

### Full Mode (~3–5 days per layer)
- Original primary research per layer (interviews, forums, academic literature)
- 25–40+ sources per layer
- Produces detailed research documents + synthesis + cross-layer analysis
- Best for: new product from scratch or a major strategic pivot

---

## Output Artifacts

For each layer, you produce:
1. **Research document(s)** — raw findings, sources, quotes
2. **Layer synthesis** — consolidated findings, key insight, strategic implication

After all six layers:
3. **Cross-layer insights** — convergences across layers, unified barriers
4. **Problem statement** — one paragraph, grounded in all six layers
5. **Solution direction** — what the research points toward (not a spec — a direction)

Use the templates in `templates/` to structure these outputs.

---

## Connecting to What Comes Next

The 6-Layer framework produces the *opportunity* side of Torres' Opportunity Solution Tree. After completing it you have:

- A rigorous problem definition
- A map of behavioral barriers and enablers
- An understanding of what has already been tried
- A scoped view of what's feasibly changeable

The next step (outside this skill's scope) is building the Opportunity Solution Tree — mapping opportunities discovered in the research to potential solutions, then assumption-mapping and running structured experiments.

See: [Opportunity Solution Trees — producttalk.org](https://www.producttalk.org/2023/12/opportunity-solution-trees/)

## Vocabulary

| Term | Definition |
|------|------------|
| **Opportunity** | A customer need, pain point, or desire — not a solution |
| **Mental model** | The customer's internal explanation of how something works or why they behave the way they do |
| **Status quo attempt** | Any prior solution (product, workaround, habit) that addresses the problem, however partially |
| **Feasible influence** | The subset of the problem space your team can realistically affect, given your constraints |
| **Synthesis** | A single document that distils findings from one layer into consolidated insights — not a list of raw findings |
| **Problem statement** | A one-paragraph description of the problem, written *after* all six layers, that incorporates all lenses |
